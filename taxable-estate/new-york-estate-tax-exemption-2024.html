Understanding New York estate tax laws

Navigating the intersection of federal and state estate taxes can be complex for individuals and families with significant wealth. Generally speaking, an estate tax is a levy on the assets of a deceased person prior to their dispersal to their heirs. There are different thresholds and tax rates that vary from state to state, as do the rules regarding how to calculate an estate’s value.

When considering your estate plan, it’s important to remember that the value of an estate is calculated as the sum of all property you own at the time of death, minus any outstanding debts. This includes cash, investments, real estate, and personal property like jewelry, antiques, and vehicles. In addition, certain tax-exempt entities (like charitable organizations and government agencies) are not included in your estate.

In addition, it is important to understand that New York State has its own estate tax and a “cliff” that applies when the value of an individual’s taxable estate exceeds $6.11 million in 2024. The New York estate tax exemption 2024 is called the unified credit, and it can be used to offset the federal estate tax rate.

New York also has its own gift tax, which is a tax that is applied to gifts made during an individual’s lifetime. This can be a valuable tool to reduce the overall size of an estate, but it’s important to work with your advisor and tax and legal professionals to ensure that your gifting strategy is coordinated with your overall estate plan.

Another consideration to keep in mind is that New York does not allow for the portability of an estate’s exemption between spouses, so any unused exemption will be lost upon the second spouse’s death. This is one reason why it’s important for couples to work with their advisors to develop a holistic approach to their wealth planning, especially in light of the New York estate tax exemption 2024.

Key aspects of New York estate tax

The New York estate tax is a state-level tax levied on the transfer of wealth upon a person's death. Understanding the intricacies of this complex tax system, including the New York estate tax exemption 2024, is vital to estate planning and administration, as it can help ensure that assets are transferred to heirs and beneficiaries in the most tax-efficient manner.

The key aspects of New York estate tax include the tax threshold, rates, and deductions. Estate taxes are levied on the total value of a deceased individual's assets minus allowable exemptions and deductions. New York's estate tax is a progressive tax, meaning that the rate increases as the value of the taxable estate rises. The New York estate tax threshold is currently $6.94 million, which is lower than the federal estate tax threshold of $11.18 million. Using New York's Exemption Trust

Since the state's exemption is not portable between spouses ("use it or lose it"), it's important for couples to preserve their exemption by using New York's exemption trust. This is a special type of trust that is established with an estate's federal and New York exemption amounts. The funds in this trust are not considered part of the taxable estate for purposes of New York estate tax, and they can remain in the trust for life, growing with interest.
